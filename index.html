Harsh Treatment

A year ago, Medill Watchdog at Northwestern University and the Chicago Tribune undertook an unprecedented look inside residential treatment centers designed to help youth with severe emotional, behavioral or mental issues.

There are hundreds across the country. Some are run by non-profits; some by religious organizations; some by for-profit corporations. Some youth are sent there by exasperated parents seeking the best setting for their children. But most commonly these facilities are funded by tax dollars, paid to them by school districts and youth social welfare agencies, after young people are placed there as the best option to help them.

Over the past year, Medill Watchdog interns filed requests for public records from local law enforcement and state and county agencies, to get a picture of the kinds of problems that the facilities are facing. The responses were uneven—some places refused our requests altogether, or said they would only provide them for fees running into thousands of dollars.

But the Medill Watchdog and Chicago Tribune team accumulated a massive number of records—more than 66,000 pages of responses—and then reviewed lawsuits, news accounts and corporate records, as well as conducting hundreds of interviews with former residents, their parents, staff members and officials from the institutions, police officers, state officials and outside experts in the care of disturbed children.

Harsh Treatment: Medill Watchdog exposes dangers in centers for troubled youth from medillwatchdog on Vimeo.

What emerges is the most comprehensive look inside the residential treatment centers nationwide ever conducted. Running these facilities is no easy task. Some of the youth housed in these institutions are violent, sometimes sent to centers after criminal trouble. Caring for them as well as for children who may have been sexually abused, mentally frail or suicidal is tremendously challenging.

The quality of care, and the quality of oversight provided by officials, is uneven, the review shows. Federal efforts to create uniform standards years ago went no where, and the problems highlighted in congressional hearings in 2007 persist. Often the state and local law enforcement agencies may not even alert each other when incidents occur, making the job of protecting vulnerable youth even more difficult.

The facilities vary, based on state regulations, in how tightly they restrict youth from leaving the facilities. They vary in whether staff can physically restrain out-of-control youth, and what forms of restraint are permitted. They vary in their ability to attract and retain skilled workers.

Among the issues the Medill Watchdog/Tribune investigation uncovered:

Some facilities are plagued by youth running away, creating risks to themselves or to members of the community. Some facilities have recurring incidents in which youth are injured by restraints of staff, at times by staff not properly trained in how to physically stop a youth with minimum risk of harm. Some facilities are plagued by excessive incidents of residents assaulting each other, including sexual assaults. Former staff in many facilities complain that the pay is too low, the training inadequate, and the number of staff too few to keep up with the youth.

And the investigation turned up evidence that some facilities are slow to alert authorities to problems, or discourage their staff from giving full reports to state or law enforcement officials.

Eau Claire Academy
Eau Claire, Wisconsin

Police and state reports from the last three years show that Eau Claire Academy was plagued by high numbers of runaways and violence directed at both residents and staff. Staff members say they were under-equipped to deal with residents and that they were often required to rewrite incident reports to make them seem less problematic. Foundations For Living
Mansfield, Ohio

M edill Watchdog found troubling issues plaguing Foundations For Living, an 84-bed facility in Mansfield, Ohio, for youth 11 to 17 years old struggling with severe psychological, behavioral and addiction problems. Youth fought each other; they tried to runaway; they attacked staff members who were there to keep order. Read on...

Provo Canyon School
Provo, Utah

Since 2011 police have responded more than once a week, on average, to one of the three campuses that collectively make up Provo Canyon School, which collectively can care for 274 youth at any time. The calls include at least 56 reports of assault and 25 reports of sex offenses. Read on...

"He was pushed into the corner and punched over and over."
Wolverine Human Services (Vassar Campus)
Vassar, Michigan

Younger, more vulnerable residents have often been brutalized by older, tougher residents at Wolverine Human Service's Vassar, Michigan campus. Juvenile felons—some of them former gang members—have preyed on vulnerable abuse and neglect victims. They have instigated dangerous inter-bunk brawls and posed a serious threat to staff and kids alike. Read on...

"Staff is supposed to protect kids. They didn't protect him."
Citrus Health Network
Pembroke Pines, Florida
Youth at Citrus Health Network's South Florida residential treatment center have on many occasions engaged in violence against each other; staff efforts to quell trouble has at times raised its own issues. The facility's treatment of teenagers with mental and emotional problems captured widespread attention after the television broadcast of one incident captured on video.

The January 2014 death of 15-year-old Kenneth Barkley was neither the first nor the last incident in which an OhioGuidestone resident was injured during a restraint, records from the Berea Police Department and Ohio Department of Mental Health and Addiction Services show. At times, restraints ended in significant injury or, as in the case of Barkley, even death.

Alexander Youth Network | Charlotte, North Carolina

Children treated at Alexander Youth Network were sometimes sexually abused by other children or staff. One staff member went to prison for committing sexual acts with a resident. Also, staff was unable to determine the cause of a bloody head injury inflicted on one mentally delayed child. Read more...

Boys and Girls Home of Alaska | Fairbanks, Alaska

State inspectors expressed concern about staff's ability to keep children safe from harm. Incidents included staff hitting a child in the face, performing oral sex on a 12-year-old boy and pouring water on a restrained child. The inspectors also cited staff for improper training after a suicide attempt. Read more...

Campbell Lodge Boys' Home | Cold Spring, Kentucky

Campbell Lodge closed after a 2012 state investigation found repeated incidents of staff abusing and neglecting residents. Inspection reports from the Kentucky Cabinet for Health and Family Services detailed one incident in which a staff member allegedly grabbed a child by the neck and forced him to the ground. Read more...

Devereux Florida (Orlando Campus) | Orlando, Florida

The Residential Treatment Center at the Orlando Campus of Devereux Florida exhibited a host of problems leading to resident harm. Medill Watchdog found 80 cases of battery at the facility, often involving underage residents and resulting in 52 cases of resident arrest and transport to juvenile detention centers. Read more...

Devereux Georgia Treatment Network | Kennesaw, Georgia

State agencies cited the Devereux Georgia Treatment Network on numerous occasions for violations of state regulation, including staff negligence resulting in a sexual assault and failing to provide specialized treatment or services for a resident whose admission assessment made clear the need for close supervision due to a history of inappropriate sexual behavior. Read more...

Family & Children's Center | La Crosse, Wisconsin

Family and Children's Center was cited for lack of supervision for serious incidents including children trying to commit suicide, harming themselves and engaging in sexual activity. State inspectors found the center to be lax in reporting and documenting incidents. Police reports revealed that children at the facility faced criminal charges for minor infractions. Read more...

Gibault Children's Services | Terre Haute, Indiana

Terre Haute community members complained about Gibault Children's Services after a series of incidents in which youth left the facility and posed risk to themselves and neighborhood residents. The most serious incident involved walkaways' assault of community members, auto theft and a police chase that ended in a crash that killed one of the youth. Read more...

Lives Under Construction Boys Ranch | Lampe, Missouri

Two former residents await trial on charges that, days after they escaped from Lives Under Construction Boys Ranch in January 2013, they murdered a couple vacationing nearby during a botched robbery. The Stone County Sheriff's Office reported that one of the boys confessed to beating and stabbing the couple. Read more...

The Manor | Jonesville, Michigan

The Manor was plagued by a variety of problems so severe the state of Michigan closed the facility in March 2013. The state initiated more than 100 investigations between 2009 and the facility's closing, nearly 60 of which resulted in licensing violations. Read more...

TOP

Maryhurst's Euphrasia Treatment Program | Louisville, Kentucky

A Kentucky state agency cited Maryhurst's Euphrasia Treatment Program for several cases of improper restraint and a lack of adequate supervision of residents between 2011 and 2012. In one incident, a staff member pushed a child into a time-out room, pushed her to the ground, then lay on top of her, state reports show. Read more...

Parmadale Institute | Parma, Ohio

Catholic Charities shut down the Parmadale Institute in early 2014 following a series of issues, including staff on resident sexual assault, led the state to place the facility on probation. In 2008, a state investigation found fault with the conduct of staff members who applied a restraint to a resident, resulting in her death. Read more...

Red Rock Canyon School | St. George, Utah

A staff member at Red Rock Canyon School pleaded guilty in 2014 to three counts of forcible sex abuse charges involving one boy, 13, and two who were 16. Following the staff member's arrest, a state licensing investigation found the facility had violated several rules, including failing to report the sexual abuse. Read more...

St. Cloud Children's Home | St. Cloud, Minnesota

St. Cloud Children's Home was cited repeatedly by the state for a series of incidents where inadequate staff supervision resulted in sexual activity among residents. The reports blamed these incidents on lax supervision, among other issues. Read more...

Star View Adolescent Center | Torrance, California

Between January 2011 and April 2014, 13 Star View Adolescent Center staff members were fired and three more resigned after repeated incidents of failing to adequately supervise children, records from the California Department of Health and Human Services detail. Read more...

Vista Maria | Dearborn Heights, Michigan

Vista Maria has been cited repeatedly in recent years for a variety of violations, often involving staff seeking to control residents through improper restraints or other physical measures. Twice in 2014, the state cited incidents that ended in minor injuries to girls whom staff tried to move against their will. Read more...

Wyalusing Academy | Prairie Du Chien, Wisconsin

Wyalusing Academy had its childcare license revoked in 2013 after three staff members seriously injured a 14-year-old boy during a restraint. State documents also revealed other incidents of improper restraint and cruel treatment of youth at the facility in preceding years. Read more...
